Summary

This X post presents sports betting from a quantitative finance perspective, drawing from Alex Monahan's work. The core argument is that 90% of sports bettors lose because they treat betting like gambling—relying on intuition and luck—while the other 10% operate as mathematical traders using the same principles employed by hedge funds and prop trading firms.

The post emphasizes the critical distinction between true probability (the actual likelihood of an outcome) and implied probability (what sportsbooks' odds suggest). Sportsbooks embed their profit margin (the "vig" or vigorish) into every line—for example, a 50/50 event might be offered at -110/-110, which mathematically implies each side has about 52.4% probability. This means the total implied probabilities exceed 100%, creating the bookmaker's edge.

The key to profitable betting, according to this framework, is identifying positive expected value (+EV) opportunities: situations where your calculated probability of an outcome exceeds the implied probability in the odds. This transforms betting from speculation into systematic arbitrage. The post illustrates how sharp bettors exploit cross-book discrepancies, locking in profits by betting all outcomes across different sportsbooks, typically generating 5-10% ROI in minutes. It also explains that sportsbooks behave like financial market makers, moving lines in response to sharp money while public bettors pay for emotional and intuitive decisions.

The underlying thesis—that decision-making under uncertainty can be systematized through probability, statistics, and market theory—applies far beyond sports betting to trading, business, and life. The post concludes that success in the long run comes not from being right more often, but from having an "edge," a concept that requires understanding the mathematical foundation of probability rather than relying on skill in predicting outcomes.

Key Takeaways

  • The distinction between true probability and implied probability is foundational: sportsbooks build in a 'vig' (typically 4-10%+ margin) that makes most bets mathematically unfavorable unless the bettor identifies mispricings.
  • Expected value (+EV) is the core metric: a bet is +EV when your calculated probability of an outcome exceeds the implied probability in the odds, creating a mathematical edge over time.
  • Arbitrage across multiple sportsbooks can lock in guaranteed 5-10% returns in minutes by betting all outcomes at different books that have pricing discrepancies—this is trading, not gambling.
  • Sharp bettors move sportsbook lines immediately due to their consistent accuracy, while public/casual bettors move lines through sheer volume but in directions that typically favor the sportsbooks.
  • Sportsbooks operate as market makers, balancing action on both sides and profiting from the vig, not from being correct—they don't need to win every game, just maintain a positive expected value across thousands of bets.
  • The law of large numbers ensures that mathematical edge only manifests after high bet volume: a 5% edge might show losses after 50 bets but near-certain gains after 5,000.
  • Common strategies that destroy bankrolls include parlays (which amplify house edge) and prop bets (which often carry 20%+ house edge), even when individual bets are well-reasoned.
  • Alex Monahan's credibility derives from his background: Stanford CS/Math graduate, former quantitative trader at Susquehanna (top US prop trading firm), founder of OddsJam (acquired for $160M), and documented sports betting profits of $400k+.
  • The principles apply universally: identifying edge in any decision-making environment (trading, business, investing) requires the same discipline of calculating true probabilities and only acting when conditions provide mathematical advantage.
  • Sportsbooks increasingly limit or ban winning players, creating a practical constraint on scalability—the mathematical advantage doesn't guarantee real-world profit if platforms restrict account access.

Research Notes

Alex Monahan is a credible and high-profile figure in quantitative sports betting. His OddsJam platform was acquired by Gambling.com Group for up to $160 million in December 2024, and he was a working quant trader at Susquehanna International Group—one of the most respected prop trading firms in the world. This lends significant weight to his perspective. However, important caveats exist. First, while the mathematical framework described is sound and well-established in quantitative finance, the practical application to sports betting faces real-world constraints: sportsbooks aggressively limit or ban winning bettors, and arbitrage opportunities disappear within seconds as markets become more efficient. Reddit discussions in r/PositiveEVbetting note skepticism about whether Monahan's $400k+ annual claims are realistically achievable given these limitations, particularly given his dual role as OddsJam founder (which creates conflicts of interest in promoting the platform). Second, the tweet attributes the content to an MIT lecture, but I found no direct evidence of a formal MIT lecture by Monahan in my research—this may be informal content delivered at MIT or conflation with his YouTube educational videos. The broader context is important: the sports betting industry has evolved from bookmaking toward algorithmic trading. Quant trading firms are actively recruiting algorithmic bettors, and the lines between quantitative finance and sports betting have blurred significantly. However, the industry remains restricted—many bettors are banned for winning—which contrasts with more liquid financial markets. This represents an ongoing tension: the mathematical edge described in the post is real, but the market structure actively works against those trying to exploit it at scale. Monahan's educational content (YouTube videos, podcasts, X posts) has been well-received and influential in the online sports betting community, particularly among those interested in the '+EV betting' and 'sharp betting' communities.
