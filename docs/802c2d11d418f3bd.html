Summary

Naomi Oreskes and Erik M. Conway, prominent historians of science, trace 200 years of technological development to argue that the relationship between scientific discovery and technological innovation is far more complex and unpredictable than commonly assumed. Contrary to the notion that science drives technology, they show that before the 19th century, major innovations (compass, printing press, steam engine) emerged from craft traditions independently of scientific knowledge.

The authors examine six major technologies to illustrate their thesis. Three—electricity, telecommunications, and computing—genuinely transformed society in ways their creators anticipated, though often through unexpected mechanisms. Electricity, for instance, didn't just power homes; it enabled factories to relocate anywhere and enabled suburban sprawl by powering streetcars and subways. Radio and television succeeded not because they were scientifically revolutionary, but because broadcasters created compelling content that drove consumer adoption, simultaneously building mass marketing and consumer culture.

Conversely, two highly touted technologies—nuclear power and human space travel—failed to deliver on their grandiose promises. Nuclear power foundered not on scientific or engineering grounds, but on economics and safety concerns: construction and labor costs remained perpetually high, making it uncompetitive with alternatives. Human space travel, despite massive government investment and Cold War nationalism, never became routine or commercially viable because safety and cost requirements made it economically unfeasible outside military contexts.

The authors identify a crucial, largely overlooked pattern: technologies designed to move matter and energy ended up having their most profound impact on moving information. Satellites derived from space programs now enable instantaneous global telecommunications and Earth observation capabilities more valuable than any crewed missions. Computers, initially conceived as calculation machines, became primarily tools for storing, accessing, and creating information. This transformation culminated with Tim Berners-Lee's deliberate design of the World Wide Web in 1990 with universal authorship as an explicit goal.

The article concludes by highlighting the most significant ongoing transformation: the blurring of boundaries between consumers and producers of information. Historically, media flowed one-way from newspapers, radio, and television to passive audiences. Today, individuals can produce and share content globally via Instagram, Facebook, YouTube, and other platforms, fundamentally reshaping power dynamics between information creators and audiences. The authors stress that while prediction about technology's future is notoriously difficult, this shift toward democratized information production will likely continue reshaping society in ways we're only beginning to understand.

Key Takeaways

  • Before the 19th century, major innovations (magnetic compass, printing press, cotton gin, steam engine) emerged from craft traditions with minimal connection to scientific knowledge or theory.
  • Electricity's greatest impact came not from replacing manual labor, but from enabling factories to locate anywhere (removing water-power constraints), powering mass transit systems, and facilitating suburban sprawl—transforming urban geography and daily life.
  • Radio and television succeeded because broadcasters created content (news, music, drama) that drove consumer adoption, which simultaneously created advertising, mass marketing, and consumer culture—not because the technology itself was revolutionary.
  • Nuclear power failed not on scientific or engineering grounds, but because construction, materials, and labor costs remained persistently high while safety requirements increased costs further, making it uncompetitive despite government promotion and subsidies.
  • Human space travel, despite massive investment and Cold War urgency, remained too expensive and dangerous for civilian purposes; government created the technology for military and ideological reasons, not market demand, limiting its adoption.
  • The greatest payoff from space technology came not from crewed missions but from satellites, which enabled instant global telecommunications and Earth observation (weather, climate, ecosystems, GPS) more valuable than any human spaceflight achievement.
  • Computers were designed to replace human calculators but evolved to primarily store, access, and manipulate information rather than perform calculations, becoming information-management tools.
  • Tim Berners-Lee explicitly designed the World Wide Web in 1990 with universal authorship as a primary goal, enabling anyone to become both consumer and producer of information.
  • The historic one-way flow of information from media institutions to passive audiences is reversing; platforms like Instagram, Facebook, YouTube, and TikTok enable individuals to produce and share content globally, fundamentally altering power dynamics.
  • Approximately 25% of American adults lack high-speed internet access, representing a persistent digital divide that limits access to information and participation in the information economy despite the technology's widespread availability.

Sentiment / Tone

Historically grounded and intellectually nuanced, with measured skepticism toward technological determinism and utopian narratives. The authors acknowledge complexity and unintended consequences rather than presenting simple cause-and-effect stories. Their tone is neither pessimistic nor optimistic, but analytical—presenting evidence that prediction about technology is inherently difficult. They implicitly critique both techno-utopianism (the belief that technology automatically solves problems) and techno-pessimism, instead showing that outcomes depend on complex interactions between economics, politics, safety concerns, and market demand. The piece respects reader intelligence by avoiding sensationalism and instead building credibility through specific historical examples and nuanced analysis.

Research Notes

The authors—Naomi Oreskes (Harvard History of Science professor) and Erik M. Conway (NASA JPL historian)—are highly credible scholars with extensive publications on the history of science and technology. They co-authored "Merchants of Doubt" (2010), a widely-cited book investigating how scientific uncertainty was weaponized to create doubt about climate change and other issues. This background makes them particularly attentive to how scientific narratives can be shaped by political and economic interests. The article's claim that ~25% of American adults lack high-speed Internet is corroborated by Pew Research Center data and academic literature on the digital divide. Their point about information overload disproportionately affecting less-educated, poorer, and older populations aligns with research showing that those with more technology and education feel more able to manage information flows. The article builds on Ruth Schwartz Cowan's influential 1983 book "More Work for Mother," which won the 1984 Dexter Prize and showed that labor-saving household appliances (refrigerators, washers) didn't reduce women's work time—instead, they raised expectations for household cleanliness and order, demonstrating how technology can have counterintuitive social effects. This supports their broader argument that technological impacts are rarely what we anticipate. The article was published in 2020, giving it relevance to COVID-19 discussions about digital access and inequality. The point about blurring producer-consumer boundaries connects to broader scholarly discussion about Web 2.0, participatory culture, and "prosumption" (a term combining production and consumption). Tim Berners-Lee's explicit design goal for the Web—enabling universal authorship—is well-documented in his 1989-1990 proposals at CERN and reinforces the article's point that the Web's revolutionary potential for democratizing information production was intentional, not accidental. One potential caveat: the article somewhat oversimplifies the distinction between government-developed (Internet) and privately-developed (electricity) technologies. Electricity development actually benefited from significant government support through the Rural Electrification Administration, which the authors mention but don't emphasize equally. This nuance slightly undermines their government-vs-private-sector comparison, though their overall framework remains sound.
