Summary

The Turkish tech news account @deeptechtr posted about Microsoft's VibeVoice, an open-source AI voice synthesis framework, claiming that Microsoft has released a powerful AI voice tool as open source and removed security watermarks that were previously used to prevent misuse. The post, written in Turkish, suggests users no longer need to pay for premium AI voice tools and implies that the technology is now available without protective measures.

However, the claim requires significant context and fact-checking. Microsoft did indeed open-source VibeVoice in August 2025, featuring advanced text-to-speech capabilities including the ability to generate 90-minute conversations with multiple speakers and perform voice cloning from short audio samples. The framework is genuinely free and open-source. However, the assertion about removed watermarks appears to be misleading. While Microsoft initially disabled the VibeVoice-TTS repository code in September 2025 after discovering misuse (voice cloning for deepfakes), they subsequently re-enabled it with enhanced safeguards: an audible AI disclaimer automatically embedded in all synthesized audio and an imperceptible watermark for provenance verification.

The post reflects ongoing tension in the AI community around balancing open-source research access with responsible AI deployment. As of April 2026, voice cloning scams have become a significant social problem, with reported incidents of criminals using AI-generated voice impersonations for fraud targeting elderly people and families. The timing of this post coincides with increased awareness of deepfake risks, making the framing—which downplays security measures—potentially problematic from a responsible disclosure perspective. The tweet's suggestion that users can now access powerful voice synthesis without watermarks or oversight is partially inaccurate, though it correctly identifies that the tools are freely available and open-source.

Key Takeaways

  • Microsoft released VibeVoice as open-source AI voice synthesis and recognition in August 2025, including text-to-speech models that can generate up to 90 minutes of natural-sounding conversational speech with multiple speakers.
  • VibeVoice enables voice cloning from short audio samples (3-10 seconds), raising significant deepfake and fraud risks, which Microsoft discovered shortly after initial release.
  • The claim that watermarks have been 'removed' is misleading: watermarks and audible AI disclaimers are actively embedded in all generated audio on current versions, though they can theoretically be circumvented through audio editing.
  • Microsoft temporarily disabled the VibeVoice-TTS code repository in September 2025 after discovering misuse, then re-enabled it with additional safeguards including mandatory audible disclaimers and imperceptible watermarks.
  • As of April 2026, voice cloning scams are demonstrably increasing, with reported cases of AI voice impersonation used for financial fraud, family extortion, and identity manipulation.
  • The VibeVoice-ASR (speech recognition) and VibeVoice-Realtime (0.5B real-time model) are fully functional and available on Hugging Face, while the TTS code repository was removed from GitHub but models remain available.
  • The tools are genuinely free and require no payment, confirming the post's claim about cost, but the 'no watermarks' assertion misrepresents current technical safeguards.
  • Microsoft's MIT license includes explicit restrictions prohibiting fraud, impersonation, and disinformation, though enforcement mechanisms rely on technical safeguards that experts argue are circumventable.

Research Notes

DeepTechTR (@deeptechtr) is a Turkish tech news aggregator account focused on deep technology announcements and trends. The post's claim about watermark removal is partially inaccurate based on current documentation. Microsoft's official GitHub and Hugging Face repositories (as of April 2026) explicitly document that both audible disclaimers and imperceptible watermarks are embedded in generated audio files. However, security researchers have documented that both measures have limitations: the audible disclaimer can be edited out, and the imperceptible watermark "degrades under compression or adversarial processing," which Microsoft was aware of at release time. This suggests the post conflates "limited watermarks" with "no watermarks." The broader context is significant: as of April 2026, deepfake voice scams have become a documented crime vector. A US Senate Judiciary Committee memo from April 16, 2026 documents cases of voice cloning used for family extortion scams, with victims reporting being convinced by the realism of synthesized voices. This timing makes the post's framing—emphasizing free access without emphasizing safety measures—contextually problematic. Microsoft's decision to initially disable the TTS code repository in September 2025 but maintain model availability on Hugging Face represents a middle-ground approach. Community forks exist on GitHub, indicating that the gating strategy has limited practical effect on determined users.
