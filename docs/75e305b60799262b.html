The thread begins with an engaging historical narrative: in 1906, Andrey Markov analyzed 20,000 letters from Pushkin's poetry by hand to prove that dependent events (unlike his rival Nekrasov's claims) could still follow the Law of Large Numbers, establishing the mathematical foundation for modeling systems with memory-dependent state transitions. The framework identifies four key findings from Becker's research: the longshot bias where low-probability contracts are systematically overpriced; the maker-taker wealth transfer where makers earn +1.12% per trade while takers lose −1.12%; category-specific edges (Entertainment/World Events show 4.79-7.32pp gaps vs Finance's 0.17pp); and the "Optimism Tax" where takers disproportionately buy YES contracts at longshots despite NO outperforming at 69 of 99 price levels. Key Takeaways

  • Markov chains enable modeling of dependent events with memory, connecting a 1906 mathematical proof about free will to modern prediction market trading through a 120-year lineage via nuclear physics and search engine algorithms.
  • 87% of Polymarket traders lose because they rely on intuition; the top 13% win by discretizing prices into states, computing transition matrices, running Monte Carlo simulations with 10,000+ paths, and sizing positions mathematically.
  • Empirical analysis of 72.1 million trades (Becker, 2026) proves systematic wealth transfer: makers consistently earn +1.12% per trade while takers lose −1.12%, a 2.24pp gap driven by behavioral biases rather than superior forecasting.
  • The 'longshot bias' is severe: contracts priced at 1¢ win only 0.43% of the time (vs 1% implied), returning 43¢ per dollar—worse odds than Las Vegas slot machines (93¢), confirming mass overpricing of low-probability outcomes.
  • The 'YES Optimism Tax' shows a 64 percentage point performance gap at equivalent prices: 1¢ YES contracts return −41% while 1¢ NO contracts return +23%, as takers disproportionately buy affirmative outcomes regardless of actual win rates.
  • Market inefficiency varies 40× by category: Finance shows only 0.17pp maker-taker gap (nearly efficient), while Entertainment (4.79pp) and World Events (7.32pp) have massive gaps due to participant selection—probability-thinking traders in Finance vs. emotional/narrative-driven bettors in entertainment.
  • Python implementation: convert 30-60 days of price history into 10 price-state bins, compute the transition matrix by counting state-to-state movements, normalize rows to probabilities, then simulate 10,000 random walks to estimate true probability vs. market price to identify edge.
  • Execution matters as much as strategy: market orders pay the −1.12% 'taker tax' while limit orders capture the +1.12% 'maker premium'—a 2.24pp swing. The choice to be a maker or taker determines returns more than directional forecasting ability.
  • Position sizing via Kelly Criterion is critical: use quarter-Kelly (0.25× optimal bet size) rather than full Kelly to reduce volatility and account for model error, preventing catastrophic drawdowns even with a profitable model.
  • The winning strategy doesn't require superior prediction—it requires superior structure: makers profit by providing liquidity to a taker population exhibiting systematic behavioral biases (preferring YES, preferring longshots) without needing to forecast which outcome wins.
